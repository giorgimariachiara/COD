Education analysis: Female data

Turning to female deputies, we find a similar pattern of variations in educational qualifications across different legislatures. In the Costituente legislature, 12 female deputies were graduates, while 8 were non-graduates. Similar to male deputies, the number of female graduates increases in recent legislatures. For example, in the 19th legislature, there are 92 female graduates and 37 non-graduates. This suggests progress in educational qualifications among female representatives over time, albeit with smaller numbers compared to male deputies. Examining the total figures for all legislatures combined, we find that out of a total of 5,226 male deputies, 3,361 (approximately 64.28%) are graduates, while 1,865 (approximately 35.72%) are non-graduates. On the other hand, out of 883 female deputies, 572 (approximately 64.77%) are graduates, while 311 (approximately 35.23%) are non-graduates.

This finding suggests that, proportionally, women were more likely to have higher education qualifications during these specific periods. However, the data also reveals that in several other legislative periods, male deputies exhibited a higher percentage of "Graduated" representatives compared to their female counterparts. For instance, during the 1st, 4th, 5th, 7th, 8th, 9th, 10th, 11th, 12th, 13th, and 14th legislative periods, male deputies consistently had a greater representation of higher education qualifications. The analysis of educational attainment among male and female deputies in the Chamber of Deputies, legislature by legislature, provides valuable insights into the gendered dynamics of representation and academic qualifications over time.

Upon further examination of the data, it is evident that the educational backgrounds and gender dynamics within the Chamber of Deputies varied significantly across different legislative periods. In certain periods, female deputies had a higher proportion of "Graduated" representatives compared to male deputies confirming the initial hypothesis that women may need to demonstrate higher educational qualifications to be represented in political positions.

Comparing the data for male and female deputies, a consistent pattern emerges. In the early legislative periods, both male and female deputies exhibited similar proportions of "Graduated" and "Non Graduated" representatives. During the "Costituente," approximately 73.2% of male deputies and 60.0% of female deputies held higher education degrees. As we progress through subsequent legislative periods, both male and female deputies showed a gradual increase in the proportion of "Graduated" representatives, indicating a growing emphasis on educational qualifications for political leadership.

By the 19th legislative period, the latest period covered in the data, the percentage of "Graduated" male deputies reached 81.0%, while "Graduated" female deputies stood at 71.1%. The percentage of "Non Graduated" male deputies decreased to 19.0%, and "Non Graduated" female deputies decreased to 28.9%.These data suggest that female deputies tend to have a slightly lower proportion of "Graduated" representatives compared to male deputies. This observation aligns with the initial hypothesis that women may need to demonstrate higher educational qualifications to overcome gender disparities in political representation.

Additionally, the comparison reveals that both male and female deputies experienced an upward trend in educational attainment over successive legislative periods.This shift reflects a growing recognition of the importance of higher education qualifications in political leadership roles, irrespective of gender. It is noteworthy that despite this progress, both male and female deputies have faced challenges in achieving educational parity, as evidenced by the proportion of "Non Graduated" representatives remaining above 10% in several legislative periods.

Overall, the data highlights a positive trend in the educational qualifications of deputies, both male and female, with an increasing number of graduates observed in recent legislatures. While there are still disparities between male and female deputies, the proportion of graduates among both genders is relatively balanced. To gain a comprehensive understanding of the educational backgrounds of deputies, it would be beneficial to further analyze the data in conjunction with additional factors such as the parties they belong to or their roles within the Chamber of Deputies. This would provide deeper insights into the relationship between educational qualifications and political representation, shedding light on potential barriers or biases that may affect access to political positions. In conclusion, the provided data offers detailed insights into the educational profiles of male and female deputies in the Italian Chamber of Deputies, legislature by legislature. While there are variations across different legislatures, there is an overall positive trend in the number of graduates among both genders. By addressing any existing disparities and promoting equal access to education, Italy can further enhance the representation of highly educated individuals within its political landscape.
