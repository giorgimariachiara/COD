Gender and Education
Analyzing the Educational Background of Deputies
How many Members of the Chamber of Deputies, men and women, are graduated?

The third research question focuses on the educational background of deputies, both male and female, and seeks to determine the proportion of representatives who hold a higher education degree. By examining the data, we can gain insights into the educational qualifications of deputies across different legislatures and explore any disparities between genders. It is hypothesized that a significant proportion of female deputies will possess higher education degrees due to gender disparities in society, where women may need to demonstrate greater qualifications compared to their male counterparts. This is in line with the notion that women often face higher scrutiny and expectations in professional settings, leading them to pursue higher levels of education to prove their competence and eligibility for political positions.

Additionally, there is a general expectation that earlier legislative periods may have a lower number of deputies with higher education degrees compared to more recent legislatures. This hypothesis stems from historical context, as educational attainment was relatively lower in the past century compared to recent times, where higher education has become more accessible. In our analysis of male representatives, we observe distinct patterns in their educational qualifications across various legislatures. On average, male deputies have a solid representation of higher education degrees, with an average percentage of approximately 67%. The mode, which represents the most frequent value, stands at 69%, and the lowest recorded percentage of male deputies holding higher education degrees is 63%, notably occurring during the XVI and XVII legislatures. These legislatures, with a lower representation of male deputies with higher education degrees, offer

insights into the complexities of educational attainment among male representatives in specific historical contexts. Interestingly, these findings challenge our initial hypothesis that earlier legislative periods may have a lower number of deputies with higher education degrees compared to more recent legislatures. However, the presence of the legislature I, with the highest percentage of male deputies holding higher education degrees, showcases the variability in educational qualifications over different legislative periods. This indicates that historical context alone may not be the sole determinant of educational trends among male representatives.

Education analysis: Female data

Turning our attention to female representatives and considering the provided data, we uncover intriguing trends in their educational backgrounds across various legislative periods. On average, female deputies exhibit a unique profile, with an average percentage of approximately 56% holding higher education degrees. Delving into the specifics, we find variations in the data. The mode, representing the most frequently occurring value, consistently stands at 55% or 56%, indicating that these percentages are frequently observed across different legislatures. This suggests a recurring trend in educational qualifications among female representatives over time. Notably, there are instances of legislative periods where the educational profile of female deputies diverges. For instance, during the '11' legislature, we observe a significant increase, with 70% of female representatives holding higher education degrees. This suggests a potential turning point or a unique context in that legislative era. Additionally, the XIV legislature stands out as an exception with 73% of female deputies holding higher education degrees, signifying a peak in educational qualifications during that specific period. While examining the educational qualifications of female representatives, we explore the hypothesis that a significant proportion of female deputies

would possess higher education degrees due to societal gender disparities. This hypothesis was rooted in the idea that women often face greater scrutiny and expectations in professional settings. However, the data analysis reveals a more complex picture. On average, female deputies exhibit an educational profile where approximately 56% hold higher education degrees. While this percentage indicates a substantial presence of educated female representatives, it falls short of confirming the hypothesis of a 'significant' proportion. Moreover, certain legislative periods show lower percentages of female deputies with higher education degrees, such as the XIV and XV legislatures. These instances challenge the notion that a uniform trend of higher educational attainment among female representatives exists across all legislative eras. In essence, the data suggests that while gender disparities persist in society, the impact on the educational qualifications of female deputies in the political arena is multifaceted. While some female representatives do possess higher education degrees, indicating their commitment to demonstrating qualifications, the presence of variability in educational profiles across different legislative periods underscores the need for a nuanced understanding of these dynamics. Societal expectations and gender disparities may play a role, but they interact with other complex factors influencing the educational attainment of female political representatives.
